# Writing Protocol

## Scope

This protocol governs **how the prose should sound on the page**, not just how a paper should be organized. It is inferred from the supplied paper corpus and is meant to guide future LLM co-authors toward this author’s scientific-paper voice.

Use it when drafting or revising:
- introductions
- theory sections
- literature framing
- hypotheses
- measurement and data prose
- empirical strategy and identification prose
- results narration
- robustness and limitations passages
- conclusion and discussion sections

This file is most useful for decisions about:
- sentence construction
- paragraph movement
- tone and degree of confidence
- syntactic density
- transitions
- lexical texture
- argument-to-evidence linkage
- what to cut because it sounds generic, inflated, or machine-written

## Core voice

The voice is formal, analytic, and controlled. It is conceptually ambitious but linguistically disciplined. The prose does not aim for minimalism; it aims for precision under complexity. It is comfortable carrying theory, method, and interpretation in the same paragraph, but it stays readable through strong connective tissue and explicit inferential control.

The tone is confident at the level of argument and restrained at the level of evidence. The writing will say **I argue** or **this study shows** when stating the paper’s core claim, but it narrows empirical language when appropriate: **suggests**, **is in line with**, **points to**, **bolsters**, **provides evidence for**, **raises the possibility**, **should be understood as**. The distinction matters. The prose should not sound timid, but it should almost never sound absolute.

The style is theory-forward without sounding doctrinaire. It does not merely report what the literature says; it positions itself against an expectation, then specifies what is missing, then advances an argument with clear causal logic. The prose is not chatty, breezy, or performatively elegant. It is built to do analytical work.

At the sentence level, the writing tends toward medium-long and long sentences with controlled subordination. It prefers exact verbs over abstract noun stacks. It tolerates jargon when the jargon names a real analytical distinction, but it avoids gratuitous disciplinary performance. It is comfortable with repetition of a key concept across a section when that repetition tracks the argument, but it avoids verbal duplication that sounds templated.

The writing should sound like someone reasoning carefully in public, not like an assistant trying to sound polished.

## Argument structure

The underlying argumentative motion is usually:

1. open with a real puzzle or substantive question
2. establish why the puzzle matters
3. locate the relevant literature or literatures
4. identify the specific gap
5. state the paper’s argument
6. unpack the causal logic
7. clarify scope conditions or rival interpretations
8. show why the empirical strategy can speak to the theory

That structure matters, but the prose should not announce it mechanically. The reader should feel a controlled narrowing: from broad stakes, to a sharper conceptual problem, to a specific claim, to a tractable empirical test.

A recurring feature of the corpus is contrastive framing. Existing expectations are not merely mentioned; they are placed beside the paper’s argument so the reader can see what is being revised, challenged, or extended. The paper often advances by juxtaposition: one logic predicts decline, another predicts compensation; one literature studies causes, the paper studies effects; one body of work focuses on structure, the paper supplies micro-foundations.

The writing also narrows claims before evidence appears. Scope conditions, alternative explanations, interpretive caveats, and inferential threats are often surfaced early. This makes the prose sound serious rather than defensive. The reader is told what the paper can show, what it cannot show cleanly, and what kinds of evidence will speak to which part of the argument.

When drafting, preserve that sequence:
- do not jump from topic to method without an articulated theoretical problem
- do not move from literature to hypothesis without stating the paper’s argument in prose
- do not report findings without clarifying what theoretical question they adjudicate

## Section-specific rules

### Introductions

Open with a substantive problem, often in the form of direct questions. These openings work because they are concrete and argumentative, not atmospheric. They present the actual issue the paper will solve.

Within the first few paragraphs, the introduction should do four things: define the stakes, identify the gap, state the argument, and preview the empirical strategy. It should not linger in generic relevance talk. It should not spend a full page warming up.

The introduction should sound like an entry into an argument already in motion. Avoid generic “topic importance” scaffolding. Replace it with a specific tension, contradiction, or missing link.

### Theory sections

Theory sections should not read like literature summaries. They should read like structured causal reasoning. Introduce the established expectation, then the paper’s alternative, then unpack the mechanisms or channels that make the alternative plausible.

Concepts are usually introduced through use rather than formal definition blocks. Define only what needs definition for the argument to move. If a concept matters, specify what it does in the causal chain.

Alternative explanations should be taken seriously enough to matter. They should not be token paragraphs. When useful, state where the main argument and the alternative make the same prediction and where they diverge.

### Measurement and data sections

These sections should explain not just what the data are, but why the operationalization is conceptually appropriate and what it does not cleanly capture. A variable description should usually contain four pieces: the concept, the measure, the source, and the interpretive caveat.

These sections should avoid dead catalogues of variables. The reader should understand why each measure belongs in the paper’s logic.

### Empirical strategy and identification sections

Name the inferential problem directly. Then explain the identification logic in plain language before adding technical specifics. The prose should make clear what assumption is doing the work.

Threats to identification should be written as analytical problems, not bureaucratic disclaimers. Explain why each threat matters, what the paper does about it, and what uncertainty remains.

Avoid ritual method prose. Every methodological paragraph should answer a real inferential question.

### Results and interpretation

Results prose should not be a sequence of coefficient paraphrases. Begin by reminding the reader what the model tests or why the outcome matters. Then state the result, give magnitude where possible, and interpret what it suggests for the theory.

Mixed evidence should be labeled as mixed. Less precise estimates should not be stretched into clean support. Use multiple outcomes or designs to narrow interpretation when possible.

### Conclusion and discussion

The conclusion should not merely recap. It should restate the argument in light of the findings, clarify the paper’s contribution, and widen carefully to broader implications. The final movement should feel like an extension of the argument, not a summary generated after the fact.

Avoid canned endings. The final paragraph should leave the reader with a sharpened implication, not a generic statement about future research or importance.

## Paragraph rules

Paragraphs should be claim-bearing units, not containers for loosely related information. A good paragraph in this style usually begins with one of four moves:
- a direct claim
- a contrast with existing work
- a sentence that states the paragraph’s analytical task
- a sentence that introduces a mechanism or inferential problem

The paragraph should then develop that opening by doing one clear piece of work: clarifying a mechanism, situating a gap, explaining a measure, narrowing an interpretation, or linking evidence back to theory. It should not simply accumulate related facts.

The prose tends to prefer paragraphs that move through three beats:
1. state the point
2. explain or ground it
3. extract the implication

The final sentence should usually advance the argument. It can narrow the claim, open the next step, or make clear what follows. It should rarely just restate the opening sentence in different words.

Paragraph length can vary, but monotony is a danger. Consecutive paragraphs of identical size and rhythm start to sound synthetic. Vary density naturally. Some paragraphs should turn quickly; others should carry layered reasoning.

What to enforce:
- start with an argumentative sentence, not a filler transition
- keep one paragraph to one analytical job
- make the last sentence earn its place
- do not end with a generic recap if the implication is already obvious
- cut any sentence that only repeats what the reader already understands

## Sentence rules

Prefer medium-long and long sentences, but vary cadence. The prose should sound controlled, not droning. Consecutive sentences of identical length make the writing feel machine-generated. Mix longer analytic sentences with shorter pivot sentences where emphasis or transition requires it.

Use syntax to express reasoning. Subordination, apposition, and embedded qualification are common and often desirable. But every clause must do work. Do not produce long sentences out of habit alone.

Prefer strong verbs:
- argue
- show
- suggest
- document
- highlight
- indicate
- leverage
- replicate
- corroborate
- bolster
- rule out
- weaken
- sharpen
- narrow
- distinguish

Prefer concrete argumentative subjects. Avoid abstract noun piles such as:
- “the conceptualization of the operationalization of the relationship”
- “the examination of the manifestation of the variation”

Turn those back into verbs.

Use transitions deliberately, but do not recycle the same ones across every paragraph. The corpus does use words like **however**, **by contrast**, **as such**, **in turn**, **taken together**, **at the same time**, **importantly**. These work because they mark a real inferential move. They stop working when repeated mechanically.

Avoid em dashes. Prefer commas, colons, or full stops. The target style does not rely on flashy punctuation for rhythm.

Avoid heavy enumerative syntax. Do not default to `(a), (b), (c)` or `(i), (ii), (iii)` or formulaic triads. If you need to sequence mechanisms, use prose that sounds written rather than indexed.

Do not over-explain inferential steps that any disciplinary reader will already follow. Spell out what matters; skip what is obvious.

## Preferred moves

- Move from a broad question to a narrow claim quickly.
- Place the paper’s argument against an existing expectation rather than presenting it in isolation.
- Use a sentence early in a section to state exactly what that section is doing.
- Turn a literature discussion into a conceptual distinction.
- Move from intuition to mechanism to observable implication.
- Name alternative explanations explicitly, then show how the design speaks to them.
- Explain why a given measure is imperfect in a way that sharpens interpretation.
- Distinguish clearly between observational evidence, causal estimates, and suggestive mechanism evidence.
- Use a second source of evidence to probe interpretation rather than forcing one design to do everything.
- Frame the contribution as an extension, correction, or reorientation of an existing literature.
- Narrow an overbroad claim rather than padding it with generic hedging.
- Use repetition of a key concept only when it helps maintain argumentative continuity across a section.

## Avoid

Aggressively cut anything that sounds prefabricated, over-smoothed, or assistant-like.

Avoid:
- stock throat-clearing openings such as “It is important to note that,” “In today’s rapidly evolving landscape,” “This highlights the need to,” “There are many reasons why,” or “Needless to say”
- inflated novelty claims such as “for the first time,” “redefines,” “paradigm shift,” “groundbreaking,” “transformative,” unless the paper genuinely warrants that level of claim
- overly symmetrical triads and contrast templates that sound rhetorically polished but conceptually empty
- generic transitions reused across paragraphs
- sentence-length monotony
- abstract noun piles with weak verbs
- recap sentences that merely restate the paragraph instead of advancing it
- fake specificity, where the prose sounds precise but the claim is still underspecified
- over-explanation of obvious inferential steps
- canned concluding sentences that read like assistant summaries
- list-heavy prose when normal sentences would do
- formal enumerations that break argumentative flow
- repeated sentence openings or repeated transition words in adjacent paragraphs
- em dashes used as a default stylistic marker
- lazy metadiscourse such as “the following section will discuss”
- generic academic filler such as “this paper contributes to the understanding of”
- verbal duplication that sounds templated rather than deliberate
- weak “AI smoothness,” where every sentence is polished to the same texture and none creates pressure or movement

Also avoid a recurring AI failure mode: restating the same claim at three levels of abstraction without adding content. If the sentence does not sharpen, qualify, or extend the point, cut it.

## Examples

### Better opening move
When do individuals compensate for the breakdown of one form of public good provision by turning to another?

Why it works: it begins from a substantive puzzle and immediately creates analytic pressure.

### Worse opening move
In today’s complex policy environment, public goods provision remains an important topic of study.

Why it fails: generic, bloodless, and interchangeable.

### Better paragraph movement
Existing work has shown that individuals are often conditional cooperators. Yet that literature usually treats cooperation as occurring within a single institutional channel. The relevant question here is different: what happens when individuals can respond to the failure of one channel by shifting to another? Framed this way, observed noncompliance need not only erode cooperation; it may also redirect it.

Why it works: contrast, narrowing, and implication all occur in sequence.

### Worse paragraph movement
Conditional cooperation is important in the literature. It has many implications for behavior. This is relevant in the present context as well. Therefore, it is useful to think about how cooperation may change.

Why it fails: recap-heavy, generic, and inferentially slack.

### Better sentence texture
The treatment worsened respondents’ beliefs about the tax system, suggesting that they processed the disclosure as evidence of free-riding rather than as a signal of stronger enforcement.

Why it works: one sentence, one clear inferential move, no wasted scaffolding.

### Worse sentence texture
It is important to note that the treatment had an effect on how respondents viewed the tax system, which highlights the fact that they may have perceived the disclosure in a negative way.

Why it fails: throat-clearing, weak verbs, fake precision.

### Better conclusion move
Taken together, these findings suggest that perceived noncompliance can shift contributions across institutional channels rather than merely depressing cooperation overall.

Why it works: it interprets and broadens without overselling.

### Worse conclusion move
Overall, this paper has shown the importance of these issues and provides useful avenues for future research.

Why it fails: canned, generic, and empty.

## Revision protocol

When revising AI-generated prose into this style, apply the following sequence.

First, cut the stock opening. Replace generic relevance talk with a real puzzle, tension, or missing link.

Second, identify the claim of each paragraph. If a paragraph does not have one, rewrite it around a single analytical job.

Third, replace weak verbs and abstract noun clusters with concrete argumentative syntax. Turn “the examination of the relationship” into “I examine whether X changes Y.”

Fourth, remove formal enumerations and convert them into prose unless the sequence itself matters analytically.

Fifth, vary sentence length. Break up stretches of evenly sized sentences. Combine short mechanical sentences into more controlled analytic ones, and split bloated sentences that are long without being precise.

Sixth, cut recap lines. Any sentence that merely restates the prior sentence without sharpening the claim should go.

Seventh, inspect transitions. Keep only transitions that mark a real move in the argument. Replace generic connectors with sharper ones or delete them.

Eighth, downgrade inflated claims. If the prose sounds broader or more certain than the design warrants, narrow it at the point of inference.

Ninth, remove fake specificity. Whenever a sentence sounds precise, check whether it actually names a mechanism, measure, source, comparison, or inferential claim. If it does not, rewrite it.

Tenth, rewrite the ending of each section. The last paragraph should either extract the implication, set up the empirical test, or clarify the contribution. It should not sound like a summary produced on autopilot.

Final check:
- Would this sentence sound plausible in a polished assistant answer? If yes, sharpen it.
- Does each paragraph advance rather than recap? If no, cut or rewrite.
- Is the prose too symmetrical, too smooth, or too list-like? If yes, roughen it into real argument.
- Does the paper sound like a person making a case under evidentiary constraints? That is the target.
