# Altac Writing Pitch Protocol

## Purpose

This protocol is for pitching **Atlantic-style ideas essays and adjacent popular-nonfiction pieces**: serious, public-facing arguments that sit somewhere between op-ed, reported analysis, intellectual essay, and policy intervention.

It merges four kinds of guidance:

1. **The Atlantic’s current formal submission procedure**
2. **Reliable outside advice about how Atlantic editors think about pitches**
3. **General popular-nonfiction pitching craft**
4. **Your existing needs as an academic writer moving into long-form public writing**

The aim is not to imitate magazine prose mechanically. The aim is to build a repeatable system for generating, researching, framing, pitching, and revising work that is ambitious enough for a place like *The Atlantic* while still usable across other high-end venues.

---

## Scope

This protocol is optimized for:

- long-form ideas essays
- intellectually ambitious commentary
- policy-reframing essays
- hybrid reported/analytical pieces
- critical essays with a live public hook

It is **not** a protocol for:

- academic journal submissions
- book proposal querying to literary agents
- generic service journalism
- pure memoir or personal essay without a strong public frame
- straight daily-news reporting pitches

---

## How this protocol was built

I synthesized:

- The Atlantic’s official submissions/help-center guidance
- external Atlantic-adjacent advice from editor interviews and freelancer/journalism craft resources
- the uploaded materials on networking, short-nonfiction pitching, and translating academic writing into popular writing
- the prior Atlantic-specific ideas-writing protocol already developed in this project

That matters because the best pitching practice comes from **combining formal rules and informal norms**. The formal rules tell you where and how to submit. The informal norms tell you what makes an editor think: *this writer understands both us and the piece.*

---

## Core principle

A strong pitch is **not** a request for permission to write vaguely on a topic.

It is a compact editorial argument that establishes:

- **what the piece is**
- **why it belongs here**
- **why it matters now**
- **why you are well-positioned to write it**
- **why the editor should trust your execution**

The editor is not buying your interest. The editor is buying your angle, judgment, and likely deliverability.

# Part I. What you are actually pitching

## 1. Pitch stories, not topics

One of the most repeated pieces of advice in both the uploaded course materials and Atlantic-specific outside guidance is that you should not pitch “a subject area.” You should pitch a **story**, **argument**, or **frame**.

Bad:
- I want to write about AI and labor.
- I want to write something on the future of universities.
- I want to discuss automation and inequality.

Better:
- AI policy has obsessed over safety and competition while mostly refusing to confront a more basic institutional question: whether labor must remain inside production and on what terms the gains are shared.
- Universities are treating AI as a classroom-integrity problem when it is also a regime-change problem for knowledge work and professional formation.
- The debate over automation keeps asking whether jobs will disappear, but the more important question is who gets bargaining power over the architecture of machine-mediated work.

A good pitch names:
- a live issue
- a specific interpretive claim
- the distinctive turn you will make
- the stakes of that turn

## 2. The piece must feel like an intervention

A paper contributes to a literature.  
A magazine essay intervenes in a public argument.

That means the pitch should not sound like:
- a seminar paper abstract
- a literature review in miniature
- a bag of themes
- a promise to “explore” something without arriving anywhere

It should sound like:
- a claim entering the world
- a fresh frame on a live question
- a piece that will change how readers understand the terrain

## 3. For Atlantic-style work, the piece must offer a frame, not just a stance

For a long-form ideas essay, an opinion is not enough. The best Atlantic-type pieces usually give the reader:

- a new category
- a new distinction
- a new historical analogy
- a new institutional question
- a missing concept that clarifies the moment

That is why this genre feels larger than a newspaper op-ed. It is not just “here is my view.” It is “here is the structure we have failed to see.”

# Part II. The formal Atlantic procedure

## 4. What The Atlantic officially says

As of the current official help-center guidance, *The Atlantic* invites editorial submissions by desk. For **Ideas**, the official contact is:

- **ideas@theatlantic.com**

Their public guidance also indicates:

- general familiarity with what they publish is the best guide to fit
- manuscripts should be sent as a **Word document or PDF**
- they will generally contact you **only if they are interested in pursuing it further**

## 5. What this means in practice

At the formal level, the Atlantic process is simpler than many writers expect:

1. Identify the correct desk.
2. Send a polished pitch or manuscript to the designated address.
3. Do not expect acknowledgment.
4. Assume silence may simply mean no.

But the simplicity of the formal process hides the real difficulty: **fit and distinction**.

The hard part is not finding the email address. The hard part is sending something that feels legible as an Atlantic piece.

## 6. A practical operating rule

Use the official address as the baseline, but keep a distinction between two cases:

### Case A. Formal desk submission
Use the desk address and follow official instructions exactly.

### Case B. Targeted editor outreach
If you have a strong reason to believe a specific editor is the right person, and you have located a legitimate professional contact route, outside pitching norms suggest that targeted outreach is acceptable so long as:
- you have done real homework
- you are not ignoring explicit submission rules
- your pitch makes clear why you chose that editor
- you are not spamming or mass-blasting

In other words: obey the official process, but do not confuse “official process” with “generic slush thinking.”

# Part III. What the reliable outside sources add about The Atlantic

## 7. The unwritten Atlantic standard

The strongest outside Atlantic-specific advice comes from editor interviews and reputable journalism/freelancer resources. The recurring lessons are consistent.

### The pitch should itself demonstrate the writing
Do not merely summarize your intelligence. The pitch should show it.

An editor should be able to infer from the pitch:
- how you think
- how you frame
- whether you can sustain a serious public voice
- whether you understand narrative and stakes

### The pitch should tell a story, not just announce a concept
Even for an ideas piece, there needs to be movement. The pitch should contain some combination of:
- a contradiction
- a drama
- a tension
- a missing question
- a moment that forces a larger insight

### The pitch should make the stakes visible
The editor has to grasp:
- why this matters to the publication’s readers
- why it matters now
- what is at risk if the issue is misunderstood

### The pitch should reflect publication-specific audience awareness
For The Atlantic in particular, the question is not just “is this smart?” but:
- is this the right kind of smart?
- does it balance seriousness and readability?
- does it connect analysis to larger civic, moral, historical, or institutional stakes?
- does it offer context and perspective rather than mere punditry?

### The pitch should show that you have already thought beyond the vague thesis stage
Pre-reporting, prior reading, conceptual clarity, and a concrete sense of the argument all matter. The editor wants to know you are not improvising from a hunch.

## 8. The key Atlantic implication

For *The Atlantic*, your pitch should usually do **five jobs at once**:

1. **Name the public argument or anxiety**
2. **Identify what current debate is missing**
3. **State your frame or concept**
4. **Demonstrate that the piece can carry narrative or intellectual drama**
5. **Explain why Atlantic readers should care specifically**

If one of those pieces is missing, the pitch will often feel either too academic, too generic, too newsy, or too thin.

# Part IV. General popular-nonfiction pitching system

## 9. The full workflow

The uploaded materials are especially useful because they do not treat pitching as only “write one email.” They treat it as a sequence.

A strong workflow is:

1. generate the story idea
2. decide why you are qualified to write it
3. identify the right publication and editor
4. research them hard
5. shape the idea into a specific editorial proposition
6. draft the pitch
7. send it strategically
8. track, follow up, negotiate, and maintain the relationship

Treat pitching as editorial targeting plus craft plus business discipline.

---

## 10. Step 1: generate an idea with editorial shape

A publishable idea usually starts where three things overlap:

- **your obsession**
- **public relevance**
- **publication fit**

The most useful generative questions from the uploaded materials are still the right ones:

- What is obvious to me that is not obvious to everyone?
- What do I understand unusually well?
- What would surprise a broad but educated reader?
- What does everyone keep describing in the wrong terms?
- What current event or anxiety can this idea illuminate?
- What topic becomes genuinely more interesting when filtered through my expertise?

For public-facing work, “interesting to me” is not enough. The idea must survive translation into:
- story
- argument
- relevance
- audience

## 11. Step 2: believe you are qualified, but define qualification correctly

The uploaded “seven steps” note is right to insist on this. Many writers, especially academics new to public writing, under-qualify themselves psychologically and overcompensate rhetorically.

Qualification is not:
- having already written ten pieces on this exact topic
- having a perfect prior byline trail
- being the final authority in the field

Qualification is:
- having real knowledge or access
- having a distinctive interpretive angle
- being able to execute the argument or reporting credibly
- having some reason the editor should trust you with this piece

Possible forms of qualification:
- subject-matter expertise
- direct reporting access
- unusual institutional vantage point
- previous related writing
- a hybrid position that lets you translate one world to another

The pitch should not grovel. It should establish credible authority without overinflation.

---

## 12. Step 3: research the publication and editor

This is one of the strongest parts of the uploaded course materials, and it is easy to underrate.

Never pitch without learning:
- what the publication publishes
- what it does not publish
- typical length and tone
- relevant sections or verticals
- whether unsolicited submissions are explicitly welcomed, limited, or discouraged
- who the likely acquiring editor is
- what that editor has recently edited, written, commissioned, or shared

### Research hierarchy

#### First: the publication itself
Read:
- section pages
- masthead
- submission guidelines
- recent pieces in the relevant lane

#### Second: the editor
Look at:
- bylines
- masthead role
- social feeds
- past work
- interviews
- calls for pitches
- the kinds of writers and pieces they have clearly championed

#### Third: your network
See whether you have:
- a mutual contact
- a former colleague with an introduction path
- a writer friend who knows rates, preferences, or editorial style
- any soft signal about whether this editor is especially receptive to a certain type of work

The point is not parasocial excess. The point is to escape generic pitching.

## 13. Start with the right editor, not the highest-ranking one

In a multi-editor publication, the default target is usually:
- the relevant **section editor**
- not the editor-in-chief
- not the fanciest person on the masthead
- not a general inbox unless the outlet explicitly routes through one

The exception is when:
- the publication officially directs all pitches through a desk address
- you have a specific personal connection higher up
- the section structure is unclear

---

## 14. Skip the slush-pile mindset when possible, but do not become sloppy

The uploaded advice is blunt and mostly correct: the least specific inbox is usually the least desirable route.

But “bypass the slush pile” should not be misread as “ignore process.”

Better rule:
- follow formal rules
- identify the human decision-maker when possible
- do not mass-email
- do not send a generic pitch to twenty people
- do not contact people who are obviously the wrong fit

---

## 15. Social media is for research, not usually for pitching

Do not pitch by DM unless:
- the editor explicitly invites DMs
- there is a clear, public call for pitches via that channel
- you already have a relationship and know this is acceptable

Use social media to:
- understand interests
- see recent commissions
- detect seasonal calls
- learn tone and preferences

# Part V. Anatomy of the pitch email

## 16. Ideal length

A very good general default is:

- **300-500 words** for many short nonfiction pitches

But for Atlantic-style long-form ideas work, the true rule is:

- as short as possible
- as long as necessary

A good practical range is:

- **350-650 words** for most serious ideas pitches
- slightly longer only if the piece is reporting-heavy or conceptually complex

What matters is not raw word count. What matters is whether every paragraph does work.

## 17. Subject line

Your subject line should be clear, professional, and mildly compelling.

Safe default:
- **Pitch: [working title]**

Sometimes useful:
- **Ideas Pitch: [working title]**
- **Pitch for The Atlantic Ideas: [working title]**

Do not make the subject line cute at the cost of clarity.

## 18. Salutation

Use:
- the first name the editor publicly goes by

Do not default to academic honorifics unless you have a specific reason.

## 19. First paragraph: fit plus thesis

The first paragraph should do three things:

1. establish why you are writing to this person or venue
2. indicate publication-aware fit
3. state the core piece in 1-2 tight lines

You do **not** need a long compliment. One crisp line is enough.

Bad:
- I’ve long admired your brilliant publication and would be honored to submit.

Better:
- I’m writing because your Ideas section regularly publishes essays that pair policy argument with larger institutional framing, and I’d like to pitch one in that vein.

Then the thesis:
- precise
- direct
- argument-bearing
- not padded by throat-clearing

## 20. Middle paragraph(s): the editorial case

This is the core of the pitch.

The middle should answer:
- what readers already think they know
- what is missing or surprising
- what your piece will show
- how it will unfold
- why the stakes are real

For many pitches, a good structure is:

### Move 1. Set up the obvious frame
What readers think the issue is.

### Move 2. State the missing frame
What they are not seeing.

### Move 3. Explain how your piece will make that visible
Concept, evidence, examples, reporting, or institutional analysis.

### Move 4. End on a larger question or tension
This gives the editor a sense of forward motion.

For reporting-heavy work, add:
- who you might speak to
- what access or prior groundwork you have
- what reporting you expect to do

For ideas essays, add:
- the governing concept
- the historical or institutional comparison
- the policy or civic stakes

## 21. Final paragraph: deliverability

The ending should usually include some combination of:

- estimated word count
- one sentence on why you are positioned to write it
- one or two relevant clips
- thanks for their consideration

For example:
- I imagine this as a 2,500-3,500 word essay.
- I’m a political scientist at Stanford working on X and Y.
- I’ve recently written on adjacent issues in Z.
- I’d be happy to send a fuller outline or draft if useful.

## 22. Bio

Attach or paste a **medium-length bio** under the signature unless:
- the editor knows you well
- the pitch is already happening inside an ongoing relationship

Your bio should be customized. It is not a universal résumé blob.

Highlight:
- what is most relevant to this piece
- recognizable institutional position or bylines
- adjacent work
- not every credential you possess

# Part VI. What the pitch must communicate

## 23. The five indispensable questions

A serious pitch should answer all five:

### 1. What is the piece?
Genre, scope, and basic claim.

### 2. Why this publication?
Not just prestige. Actual editorial fit.

### 3. Why now?
News hook, public anxiety, or timeliness of interpretation.

### 4. Why you?
Knowledge, access, expertise, or point of view.

### 5. Why will readers care?
Human stakes, civic stakes, moral stakes, institutional stakes, or interpretive payoff.

If the pitch cannot answer these clearly, the piece is not ready.

## 24. Have an ask

One of the best lines in the uploaded materials is that a pitch must **have an ask**.

That means the email should leave no ambiguity about what you want:
- to pitch a specific essay
- of a particular kind
- for this outlet
- at an approximate length
- with a clear argument

The ask is not:
- “would love to connect”
- “wondering if this might interest you”
- “just floating an idea”
- “would love your thoughts”

You are pitching. Pitch.

## 25. Be brief and compelling

Two separate standards:
- brief
- compelling

A short but vague pitch fails.
A smart but bloated pitch fails.
The right target is concentrated energy.

## 26. Do not apologize

Do not apologize for:
- being new
- trying an idea
- “not knowing if this is right”
- “just thinking out loud”
- “sorry if this is the wrong person”

Confidence need not be theatrical. It does need to be visible.

# Part VII. What not to send

## 27. Do not usually send a full draft in the first cold email

The uploaded materials are especially clear here, and the advice is good.

Even if you have a full draft, the default move is:
- pitch the piece
- include a few strong paragraphs or a crisp summary if useful
- offer to send more

Why this matters:
- it keeps the editor reading
- it preserves editorial openness
- it avoids forcing immediate total judgment on a stranger
- it helps the editor imagine shaping the piece

### Exception
If the publication’s instructions explicitly request a full manuscript or attachment, follow the publication’s instructions.

This is especially relevant for *The Atlantic*, whose official guidance says manuscripts may be submitted as a Word document or PDF. That does **not** mean every cold idea pitch must lead with a long attachment. It means attachments are formally acceptable when you are submitting a manuscript or full piece. In practice, many editors still appreciate a concise pitch email that makes the case before asking them to open something substantial.

## 28. Do not attach random files unless there is a reason

Default rule:
- no uninvited attachment from an unknown sender

Exception:
- the outlet’s submission rules explicitly invite it
- you are clearly sending a manuscript
- the editor requested material

## 29. Do not pitch via social message unless invited

Keep the pitch in the medium editors can process professionally: email.

## 30. Do not mass-blast

Tailored beats broad.
Ten smartly targeted pitches beat fifty generic ones.

# Part VIII. Translating academic writing into popular nonfiction

## 31. The main translation problem

Academic writing often rewards:
- density
- qualification
- literature positioning
- method exposition
- depersonalization
- abstraction before narrative

Popular writing more often rewards:
- clarity
- momentum
- scene or argument early
- plain but exact language
- voice
- stakes
- relevance

Your goal is **not** to become simplistic.
Your goal is to become:
- more legible
- more vivid
- more selective
- more reader-aware

## 32. What to preserve from academic training

Do not throw away your comparative advantages.

Keep:
- argument before ornament
- inferential discipline
- conceptual precision
- research fluency
- seriousness
- institutional explanation
- resistance to generic prose

These are assets in high-end public writing when translated properly.

## 33. What must change

### Replace literature review with public problem framing
Do not open by mapping a debate among scholars.
Open by naming a contradiction, anxiety, event, or institutional failure.

### Replace method talk with credibility cues
Do not explain your research design unless the piece is about evidence quality.
Instead say:
- recent labor initiatives point in this direction
- years of work on X suggest
- reporting across Y and Z reveals
- comparative cases make visible

### Replace abstraction-first with example-plus-frame
Bring in:
- a case
- a scene
- a specific person
- a news event
- a concrete institutional fact

early.

### Replace polysyllabic self-defense with accessible precision
Use the best word, not the most ornamental one.

### Replace detached authority with voice
Popular writing often sounds more like a sharp person talking than a faceless institution lecturing.

## 34. Lead with the story

Even argument-led essays benefit from narrative pressure.

For ideas pieces, “story” can mean:
- a scene
- a paradox
- a policy contradiction
- a turning point
- a revealing example
- a historical moment that crystallizes the present

Story is not decoration. It is often the delivery mechanism for the concept.

## 35. Use a public register, not a seminar register

Bad seminar habits in pitches:
- “I seek to explore…”
- “this piece will interrogate…”
- “I am interested in examining…”
- “the literature suggests…”

Better public versions:
- “This essay argues…”
- “The debate misses a more basic question…”
- “What looks like X is really Y.”
- “We are treating this as A when the real issue is B.”

## 36. Use links, not footnotes, in your mental model

Even when your thinking is built from research, the pitch should imply a public-facing sourcing logic:
- accessible references
- visible facts
- not archival apparatus

You are not signaling rigor by sounding like a bibliography.

# Part IX. Atlantic-specific adaptation

## 37. What makes an Atlantic-style pitch different from a generic essay pitch

A generic commentary pitch can sometimes get by on:
- a sharp opinion
- a timely hook
- a clean policy point

An Atlantic-style pitch generally needs more:
- an interpretive frame
- an intellectual handle
- broader stakes
- stronger sense of the essay’s world
- a voice that is serious but readable
- some reason the piece belongs in a magazine of ideas rather than a daily-opinion slot

## 38. The Atlantic-specific triangle

For a strong Atlantic-style pitch, aim to balance three elements:

### A. Concept
A memorable frame, category, or missing question.

### B. Public stakes
Why the issue matters for civic life, institutions, democracy, social order, or human flourishing.

### C. Readability
Clear, alive prose. No jargon haze.

If one side is weak:
- concept without readability becomes seminar-ish
- readability without concept becomes magazine fluff
- concept plus readability without stakes becomes clever but minor

## 39. A useful Atlantic formula

A strong Atlantic-type idea pitch often sounds like:

> We keep treating **X** as the central problem. But the deeper issue is **Y**. This matters now because **Z**. My essay would argue that the right frame is **A**, show it through **B**, and end by suggesting **C**.

Examples of what this does:
- enters a live debate
- reframes it
- names the essay’s conceptual payoff
- promises structure and stakes

## 40. Think “bigger than an op-ed, tighter than a think-tank memo”

The pitch should suggest:
- breadth of implication
- conceptual ambition
- editorial shape
- practical intelligence

Not:
- endless complexity
- policy white paper sprawl
- purely personal reflection
- trend-piece vagueness

# Part X. Recommended pitch structure for Atlantic-style ideas essays

## 41. Default template

### Paragraph 1: why this venue + thesis
One sentence of fit.  
One to two sentences of claim.

### Paragraph 2: what current debate misses
State the prevailing frame and the missing frame.

### Paragraph 3: what the essay would do
Explain the architecture:
- key examples
- institutional mechanism
- historical contrast
- reporting or expertise base
- stakes

### Paragraph 4: why you + length + clips
One sentence on authorial fit.  
Approximate word count.  
Relevant bylines or credentials.

That is the skeleton. The style and emphasis can vary.

## 42. Optional additions

### Use a mini-scene if the piece is story-led
Useful when:
- you have a striking opening anecdote
- the piece is driven by a person, place, or event

### Use a sharper conceptual sentence if the piece is frame-led
Useful when:
- the essay’s power lies in naming the problem cleanly
- the publication is particularly ideas-oriented

### Use limited sample paragraphs if your prose is a selling point
Only if:
- they are excellent
- they clarify tone and argument
- they do not bloat the email

# Part XI. Timeliness, seasonality, and follow-up

## 43. Timeliness

For news-pegged or event-pegged pieces:
- make the “why now” legible fast

For evergreen essays:
- replace pure news value with broader urgency or relatability

Not everything needs to be a hot take. But every pitch needs some answer to:
- why this moment
- why these readers
- why this editorial slot

## 44. When to send

Useful general practice:
- send during business hours
- avoid late Friday unless timeliness forces it
- remember August and holiday periods may be slower

## 45. Follow-up

General rule:
- if very timely, follow up sooner
- if not highly timely, a follow-up after about 1-3 weeks is reasonable unless guidelines say otherwise

A good follow-up is short:
- polite
- specific
- not guilt-inducing
- not repeated endlessly

If silence continues, move on.

## 46. Simultaneous submissions

Best practice:
- check outlet norms
- avoid broad, undisclosed simultaneous mass pitching
- disclose if a piece is truly time-sensitive and being sent concurrently

For prestige venues and carefully tailored ideas pieces, serial targeting is often more strategic than blasting.

# Part XII. Money, scope, and professional conduct

## 47. Know what the piece is worth

The uploaded materials are right to treat payment as part of the process, not an awkward afterthought.

Once interest is real, you need to know:
- expected range
- word count implications
- reporting burden
- turnaround speed
- rights if relevant
- kill fee if contract-based

Use:
- peer knowledge
- public pay databases where useful
- your own prior rates
- the actual labor demanded by the piece

## 48. Bring up money at the right moment

Not in the first sentence of the first cold pitch.  
But also not after the piece is effectively done.

The practical window is:
- after interest
- before substantial unpaid drafting
- before contract signature
- before publication is imminent

## 49. Be realistic about scope

One of the most useful uploaded rules:
- editors like shorter, lower-risk pieces from newer writers
- long, sprawling first-person epics are hard sells
- specific proposed length communicates professionalism

For Atlantic-style essays, length may run longer than many general web essays, but that does not excuse vagueness. If you pitch 2,500-3,500 words, know why. If you pitch 4,000+, know why even more.

## 50. Respect the promised word count

Treat word count like:
- deadline
- meeting time
- scope discipline

Running materially long shifts labor onto the editor and signals weak control.

# Part XIII. Relationship strategy

## 51. Publishing is relational, but not mystical

A recurring theme in the uploaded materials is that relationships matter. This is true. But the correct lesson is not “network performatively.” It is:

- do favors
- be reciprocal
- thank people
- share information
- be legible
- be professional
- build a reputation for seriousness and generosity

## 52. Use connections well

If you have a mutual:
- get permission before name-dropping
- an e-introduction is better than casual reference
- only use a connection that is actually relevant

## 53. Keep records

Track:
- title
- outlet
- editor
- date sent
- version
- simultaneous or not
- status
- follow-up date
- rate
- invoice/payment if relevant

This is not glamorous, but it is part of being a professional.

# Part XIV. A practical protocol for your own use

## 54. Before writing the pitch

Complete this mini-worksheet.

### The piece
- What is the exact claim?
- What is the live issue?
- What is the missing frame?
- What is the one-sentence version?

### The publication
- Why this venue?
- What recent pieces feel adjacent?
- What typical length and tone does it favor?
- Which section or desk is right?

### The editor
- Who is the likely decision-maker?
- What have they recently edited/written?
- What suggests they would care?

### You
- What qualifies you?
- What access, expertise, or point of view is distinctive?
- What clips are most relevant?

### The pitch
- What is the sharpest subject line?
- What is the cleanest working title?
- What is the simplest “why now” sentence?
- What is the clearest explanation of stakes?

If you cannot answer these quickly, the idea is probably not ready to pitch.

---

## 55. Drafting checklist

Before sending, ask:

1. Have I pitched a **piece**, not a topic?
2. Is the thesis visible within the first few lines?
3. Is the publication fit explicit but not over-flattering?
4. Does the email show how the piece would move?
5. Is there a clear why-now?
6. Is there a clear why-me?
7. Have I named approximate length?
8. Did I cut apology, vagueness, and filler?
9. Would a busy editor understand the pitch after one pass?
10. Does the prose sound like the piece deserves to exist?

---

## 56. Atlantic-specific checklist

Before pitching The Atlantic, ask:

1. Does this piece offer a frame, not just a stance?
2. Does it feel larger than a newspaper op-ed?
3. Does it connect a live issue to broader institutional, civic, or human stakes?
4. Can I explain in one sentence why Atlantic readers would care?
5. Does the pitch itself sound serious, lucid, and magazine-ready?
6. Does the piece feel like an essay of ideas rather than a policy memo?
7. If it is policy-heavy, have I preserved readability and drama?
8. If it is personal or anecdotal, have I built a larger public argument around it?
9. Is the title/deck logic strong enough to suggest editorial viability?
10. Could this plausibly sit next to the kinds of Ideas essays I admire there?

# Part XV. Reusable pitch template

## 57. General template

**Subject:** Pitch: [Working Title]

Hi [First Name],

I’m writing because [brief, specific fit line about the editor/section/publication]. I’d like to pitch a [genre/form] tentatively titled **[Title]**. The piece argues that [1-sentence thesis]. While many readers currently see this as [prevailing frame], I argue that the deeper issue is [missing frame].

The essay would show this by [brief architecture: examples / reporting / conceptual move / institutional mechanism]. In particular, it would [explain what the piece will clarify, challenge, or reveal]. The core stakes are [why this matters now, and why readers should care].

I’m well-positioned to write this because [expertise/access/background]. I imagine the piece at roughly [word-count range]. Relevant clips include [one or two links/bylines], and I’d be happy to send a fuller outline or draft if useful.

Thank you for your consideration.

Best,  
[Name]

[medium-length bio]

# Part XVI. Application to your current kind of project

## 59. For a big policy-ideas essay such as the AI labor-share proposal

Your strongest pitch lane is usually:

- not “AI is changing everything”
- not “here is my view on jobs”
- not “here is a policy proposal”

It is:

- **current debate has focused on the wrong level**
- **the real missing question is institutional**
- **the essay names the new settlement this era requires**

For your AI grand-bargain project, for example, the most compelling pitch posture is likely something like:

- existing AI policy debates concentrate on safety, innovation, and competition
- but they largely avoid the question of whether labor must remain a required input into AI-mediated production and how gains must be shared
- the essay argues for treating this as a production-regime question rather than a downstream fairness patch
- the piece sketches a concrete institutional architecture and explains why bargaining and enforcement must occur at infrastructure chokepoints rather than solely at individual firms

That is stronger than a generic “future of work” pitch because it:
- names the omission
- gives the piece a conceptual handle
- sounds like an ideas essay, not a standard op-ed
- allows you to use your comparative advantage in institutional explanation

## 60. Your comparative advantage as a writer

For this genre, your edge is not celebrity or journalist-brand familiarity. It is:

- the ability to identify missing institutional questions
- the ability to connect empirical and normative claims
- the ability to explain mechanisms
- the ability to convert policy or political-science insight into public argument

So the pitch should lean into that.  
Not with jargon. With clarity.

# Part XVII. Common failure modes

## 61. Topic without argument
The pitch names a domain but not a claim.

## 62. Argument without fit
The pitch might be smart, but gives no sense of why this outlet is the right home.

## 63. Fit without distinction
The pitch sounds like imitation rather than necessity.

## 64. Smart but bloodless
No stakes, no motion, no drama.

## 65. Academic fog
Too much literature language, hedging, or abstraction.

## 66. Magazine fog
Pretty sentences, weak argument.

## 67. Overbuilt email
Too many paragraphs, too much résumé, too much context.

## 68. Underbuilt email
No clear thesis, no clear ask, no sense of what the piece will actually do.

## 69. No why-now
The editor cannot tell why this is urgent or timely.

## 70. No why-you
The editor cannot tell why you should write it instead of someone else.

# Part XVIII. Final operating rules

## 71. The shortest durable summary

A strong Atlantic-style pitch is:

- a **targeted editorial proposition**
- for a **specific outlet and audience**
- with a **clear frame**
- a **live why-now**
- visible **stakes**
- a plausible **execution path**
- and a credible answer to **why you**

## 72. Bottom line

Do not pitch as if asking permission to think in public.  
Pitch as if offering an editor a piece that already knows why it belongs.

That means:
- do the research
- target the right person or desk
- make the claim fast
- show the frame
- make the stakes legible
- sound like the writer who can deliver it
